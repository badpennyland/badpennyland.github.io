What You Need to Get Printin'!

May 16, 2017
Supplies

So you wanna start printing at home but you aren't sure what you need, at bare minimum, to get started? Well, let me tell ya!

I highly recommend this kit from Speedball. I started out on this and was able to make my first prints right out of the box. (~$72 on Amazon). It comes with:

  • One 10"x14" (110 mesh count) screen frame
  • One 9" Squeegee
  • Two 8.5x11" sheets of transparency film
  • Four "Craft Sticks" (popsicle sticks to spread the ink across your screen)
  • Four 4oz bottles of fabric ink (black, white, red, blue)
  • One 4oz bottle of Diazo Photo Emulsion Fluid
  • One .5g bottle of Diazo Sensitizer
  • One 4oz bottle of Diazo Photo Emulsion Remover Fluid
  • One lamp with 250watt Photo Flood Bulb
  • One 9.75"x13.75" sheet of black paper
  • One useless instructional DVD and useful Instructions & Exposure Chart

This is the bare minimum you need to get printing. You can get a few of these materials elsewhere, but I feel like it's most cost effective to just get it all in this kit here.

Now that we know what we need, what are all these materials and what do they do?

THE SCREEN
Obviously you will need a screen, and 10"x14" (110 mesh count) is a great size to start with. I have since bought a smaller 8"x10" screen and some larger aluminum screens 20"x24" or so with a higher mesh count. Mesh Count is something you shouldn't worry too much about now, but in a nutshell it's just the capacity the mesh has for detail. Lower mesh counts, like 110, are great for simple, basic, and solid/thick line designs. For more detailed designs with finer lines, halftones, or photo-based, a higher mesh count is necessary to maintain a high level of detail. But unless you have a set up where you can build a light-tight exposure unit, home printing with an overhead flood lamp works best with as simple, solid designs as possible. Therefore, low mesh count is totally a-ok.

TRANSFER/TRANSPARENCY
Ok! So how the heck do you get your design onto the screen? You should consider printing a solid black (and I mean solid!) negative version of your design onto a transparency.

I do this by designing something in Adobe and printing on a transparency with my home printer. I have a normal home Brother printer. Make sure your print setting is set where it prints slow, maybe lower or normal quality setting. I find if the print setting is normal, it will create ink streaks or smudge my design. If you notice streaks or gaps in your design, you can just block out with a black Sharpie if the damage isn't too bad. Another option is to draw with sharpie or dark black ink directly on the transparency.

There is also another method called the Drawing Fluid technique where you draw the positive of your design directly onto the screen. This is not the methodology I use so I can't speak much to it, but rather offer this YouTube tutorial from Blick. You will need to purchase drawing fluid separately if you are interested in doing it this way.

EMULSION/SENSITIZER/REMOVER
This YouTube video from Speedball is super helpful! Once you coat your screen in these light-sensitive chemicals (and your screen is completely dry!), you can expose your image onto your screen. In order to activate the emulsion, you must mix the right proportion of sensitizer, which must be mixed with the right amount of water first, into the emulsion fluid. The emulsion fluid is blue, sensitizer is yellow, and when mixed properly goes on the screen as a green liquid. The kit I linked above gives you the right ratio- 4oz emulsion to .5g sensitizer. Just be mindful when you are buying replacement emulsion, you may have to buy these two separately, maybe at different quantities, so just keep the ratio in mind/read the packaging thoroughly.

When you want to reclaim a screen (use the screen for a different design) you will need the emulsion remover. I usually pour some remover on and let sit for a few minutes then rinse off with water.

LAMP/PHOTO FLOOD BULB
Dang, this is like the coolest part!! Once you have your design all set on the transparency, it's exposure time! Place the negative on your screen, with the black paper provided underneath (to assist in the exposure) and the lamp about 12" center/above your screen. I hold my lamp up using a mic stand, ha! For the 110 wood mesh frame, this kind of emulsion fluid, and the lamp method I've timed about 7 minutes of exposure time as being about right. First follow the chart on the kit, and fine-tune depending on your set up from there. Different mesh counts, different color screens (white or yellow), different brand emulsion, etc. can all be factors that can play into the exposure time. Keep a log as you fine-tune your process so you can figure out what exposure time works best for your set up.

Once you expose your screen, you will need to wash out your burned design from the emulsion. It's best to do this with some sort of pressure washer. Same goes for when it's time to claim your screens. Or just go to the local self-service car wash. :P

What's not in the kit that you might want to consider:

  • Spatula to better spread ink on screen
  • Pressure washer hose attachment, or if you are like me and in an apartment, I found this portable powerwasher to be pretty effective.
  • Some kind of base unit/way to screw hinges on your screen. You can make it yourself or buy something like this from Speedball. This is helpful if you want to set up some basic registration when printing a batch of shirts. Placement consistency, etc.

There you have it, some basic materials to get you started. In other posts I will get into more detail about additional supplies, tips, troubleshooting, things I am doing that I think are cool, etc. Stay tuned!
